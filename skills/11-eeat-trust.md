# Skill 11: E-E-A-T
## Source: Ahrefs "E-E-A-T: How to Build Trust and Boost Web & AI Visibility" (Updated Oct 2025)

---

## What EEAT Is (from Ahrefs)

"Google's E-E-A-T framework is how the search engine decides which websites to trust and show to users. E-E-A-T stands for Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness — four qualities that Google looks for when deciding if your content is worth ranking." — Ahrefs

EEAT comes from Google's Search Quality Rater Guidelines — a handbook used by human reviewers. Their feedback trains Google's algorithms. It now applies to both traditional search AND AI-driven results (AI Overviews, AI Mode).

**EEAT is not a direct ranking factor** — it's a quality signal that guides how Google's algorithms rank content. It's like reputation: you can't switch it on, but you can build it deliberately.

Google added the first "E" (Experience) in December 2022, specifically to prioritize content rooted in first-hand knowledge over generic, recycled AI content.

## The Four Pillars

### 1. Experience — Authenticity
Does the content creator demonstrate first-hand, real-world involvement?

"Experience doesn't come from expertise. You can have experience without being an expert." — Ahrefs

For tool sites — how to demonstrate experience:
- About page describing who built the tool and why
- Showing real outputs from the tool (screenshots with realistic data)
- "Last verified" dates on content where applicable (e.g., tax rates, fee schedules)
- Author bio if content covers a specific topic (e.g., "built e-commerce stores for 5 years")

### 2. Expertise — Verifiable Knowledge
Does the content demonstrate depth and skill in the specific field?

For YMYL topics (finance, health, legal): formal expertise required.
For non-YMYL topics (tools, calculators, generators): "everyday expertise" is sufficient.

Ahrefs example: Tool sites are non-YMYL. A markup calculator built by someone who runs an e-commerce business has everyday expertise. No formal credentials needed.

For tool sites — how to demonstrate expertise:
- Content is factually accurate (formulas correct, tax rates current, fee structures verified)
- FAQ section covers real edge cases — not just "what is X" but "what if Y"
- Tool handles edge cases correctly (negative numbers, zero values, large numbers)
- Supporting content goes deeper than competitors on the topic

### 3. Authoritativeness — Industry Recognition
Are other reputable sites and people referencing your site as a source?

"Authority is always relative — Tesla is an authority on electric vehicles, but not on SEO best practices." — Ahrefs

For tool sites — how to build authority:
- Directory listings (Toolify.ai, Product Hunt, There's An AI For That)
- Mentions in relevant blog posts and roundups (outreach to "best free tools" articles)
- Social profiles that have real followers and engagement
- Getting linked from adjacent authoritative sites in the same niche

### 4. Trust — The Most Important Pillar
"Trust is the most important component. A page deemed untrustworthy will have low EEAT regardless of how experienced, expert, or authoritative it appears." — Ahrefs

"Lack of EEAT is thought to be why many health and life-related websites dropped like a stone in August 2018 during the medic update." — Ahrefs

For tool sites — trust signals that must exist on every site:

**Absolutely required:**
- Clear About page — who built this, why it exists, what it does
- Working contact page — email or contact form that actually works
- Privacy policy page
- HTTPS enabled (Vercel handles this)
- No placeholder content anywhere — "Lorem ipsum", "Coming Soon", "Your content here"
- No broken links, no 404 errors on linked pages
- Tool actually works — incorrect results destroy trust instantly

**Strongly recommended:**
- "Last updated" date on tool pages where data changes (tax calculators, fee calculators)
- Transparent methodology — explain how the tool calculates results
- Clear disclosure if the site earns affiliate income ("We may earn a commission")

## EEAT for Tool Sites — Practical Reality

Ahrefs notes: "There's no shortage of websites that have completely ignored EEAT and have ranked well."

Tool sites are in the best EEAT position of any content type because:
1. The tool IS the proof of expertise — it either calculates correctly or it doesn't
2. Accuracy is directly verifiable by users — Google's quality raters can test your markup calculator with known inputs
3. No YMYL concerns — a markup calculator doesn't affect someone's health or finances in a dangerous way
4. Experience is demonstrated by building something that works

The EEAT risk for tool sites is low — but trust signals still matter. A site with no About page, no contact info, and no privacy policy raises flags. Add these on day one of every site.

## EEAT Checklist Per Site (Do Once at Launch)

- [ ] About page — who built the site, brief background, what the site offers
- [ ] Contact page — working email or contact form
- [ ] Privacy policy — simple template is fine, must be present
- [ ] HTTPS — Vercel handles this automatically
- [ ] No placeholder content on any published page
- [ ] All tools calculate correctly with known test inputs
- [ ] Tool results match external validation sources
- [ ] "Last updated" date on any page with time-sensitive data
- [ ] Social profiles exist (Twitter/X minimum) with link to site

## EEAT and AI Visibility (from Ahrefs Oct 2025)

"The framework has evolved from being a secondary guideline to the primary standard that determines the visibility of content, both in regular search results and in AI-driven results." — Ahrefs

Ahrefs cites their own data studies as proof of authority — those studies get referenced across the web and in AI responses. For tool sites, the equivalent is having tools that are:
- Cited in Reddit threads when someone asks "what's a good markup calculator?"
- Listed in "best free tools" roundups
- Mentioned by name in blog content

These citations = EEAT signals in both Google and AI systems.
