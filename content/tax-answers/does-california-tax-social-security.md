## H1

Does California Tax Social Security Benefits?

---

## ANSWER SECTION

**California does NOT tax Social Security benefits.** California is one of approximately **38 states** that completely exempt Social Security retirement, disability, and survivor benefits from state income tax. This means if you receive $2,000 per month in Social Security benefits, you pay **$0 in California state tax** on that income. However, your Social Security benefits may still be subject to **federal income tax** depending on your total combined income.

---

## H2: States That Tax Social Security Benefits

While California doesn't tax Social Security, some states do:

**States That Tax Social Security (2025):**
| State | Tax Treatment |
|-------|--------------|
| Colorado | Partially taxable (age 55-64: up to $20,000 exclusion; 65+: up to $24,000) |
| Connecticut | Exempt for income below $75,000 (single) / $100,000 (married) |
| Kansas | Exempt for AGI below $75,000 |
| Minnesota | Partially taxable (varies by income) |
| Montana | Partially taxable |
| New Mexico | Exempt for income below $100,000 (single) / $150,000 (married) |
| Rhode Island | Exempt for income below $95,800 (single) / $119,750 (married) |
| Utah | Partially taxable (flat tax with credit) |
| Vermont | Partially taxable (phases out at higher incomes) |
| West Virginia | Phasing out tax by 2026 (currently partially taxable) |

**No State Income Tax (Don't Tax Social Security by Default):**
Alaska, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, Wyoming

**States Like California (No Social Security Tax):**
All other states not listed above fully exempt Social Security benefits.

## H2: Federal Taxation of Social Security

While California doesn't tax your benefits, the federal government might:

**Combined Income Formula:**
Combined Income = Adjusted Gross Income + Nontaxable Interest + ½ of Social Security Benefits

**2025 Federal Taxation Thresholds:**

| Filing Status | 50% Taxable | 85% Taxable |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| Single | $25,000 - $34,000 | Over $34,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $32,000 - $44,000 | Over $44,000 |

**Example - Single Filer:**
- AGI: $18,000
- Tax-exempt interest: $2,000
- Social Security: $24,000 (½ = $12,000)
- Combined income: $32,000
- Result: Up to 50% of Social Security may be taxable federally

**Maximum Taxable:**
- No more than 85% of your Social Security benefits can be taxed federally
- At least 15% is always tax-free

## H2: Other California Retirement Income Taxation

While Social Security is exempt, other retirement income is taxed in California:

**Taxed in California:**
- 401(k) withdrawals
- Traditional IRA distributions
- Pension income
- Annuity distributions
- Investment income

**Not Taxed in California:**
- Social Security benefits
- Railroad Retirement benefits

**California Tax Rates on Retirement Income:**
- 1% to 13.3% depending on total income
- California does not have a special tax rate for retirement income

**Senior Tax Relief in California:**
- Senior tax credit for lower-income seniors (age 65+)
- Property tax postponement program for eligible seniors
- No special exemption for pension or 401(k) income

## H2: California Tax Filing for Seniors

**California Form 540:**
- Report Social Security benefits on Schedule CA, Line 5
- California subtraction means $0 state tax on benefits

**Important Forms:**
- Form SSA-1099 (Social Security benefits statement)
- Form 1099-R (other retirement income)
- Schedule CA (California adjustments)

**Standard Deduction (2025):**
- Single: $5,363
- Married Filing Jointly: $10,726
- Senior (65+): Additional $1,399 (single) or $1,399 each (married)
