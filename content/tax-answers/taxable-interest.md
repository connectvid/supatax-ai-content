Taxable Interest Income: What It Is and How to Report It

---

## ANSWER SECTION

**Taxable interest income** is interest you earn from bank accounts, certificates of deposit (CDs), corporate bonds, Treasury securities, and most other interest-bearing investments. Unlike tax-exempt municipal bond interest, this income is subject to federal income tax. You report taxable interest on **Form 1040, Line 2b**, and it is taxed as **ordinary income** at your marginal tax rate (10% to 37% depending on your total income).

---

## H2: Common Sources of Taxable Interest

**Bank and Credit Union Accounts:**
- Savings accounts
- Checking accounts (if they pay interest)
- Money market accounts

**Certificates of Deposit (CDs):**
- All CD interest is taxable when credited
- Even if you don't withdraw the money
- Early withdrawal penalties are deductible

**Bonds:**
- Corporate bonds
- Treasury bonds, notes, and bills (federally taxable, state-exempt)
- Series EE and I savings bonds (when cashed or matured)

**Other Sources:**
- Loans you made to others (if interest paid)
- Tax refunds (interest paid by IRS if delayed)
- Brokerage account cash sweep interest

**Not Taxable:**
- Municipal bond interest (federally exempt)
- U.S. territory bond interest (triple-exempt)

---

## H2: How to Report Taxable Interest

**Form 1040 Reporting:**
- **Line 2b:** Taxable interest
- Report total from all sources

**Source Documents:**
- **Form 1099-INT:** Interest income from banks and brokers
  - Box 1: Interest income
  - Box 2: Early withdrawal penalty
  - Box 3: Interest on U.S. savings bonds
- **Form 1099-OID:** Original issue discount
- **Brokerage statements:** Consolidated 1099s

**Reporting Threshold:**
- Banks issue 1099-INT if you earn $10+ in interest
- You must report ALL interest, even without 1099

**Schedule B Requirement:**
- File Schedule B if total interest exceeds $1,500
- Or if you have foreign accounts

---

## H2: Tax Rates on Interest Income

Interest income is taxed as **ordinary income** at your marginal rate:

**2025 Federal Tax Brackets:**

| Tax Rate | Single | Married Filing Jointly |
|----------|--------|----------------------|
| 10% | $0 - $11,925 | $0 - $23,850 |
| 12% | $11,926 - $48,475 | $23,851 - $96,950 |
| 22% | $48,476 - $103,350 | $96,951 - $206,700 |
| 24% | $103,351 - $197,300 | $206,701 - $394,600 |
| 32% | $197,301 - $250,525 | $394,601 - $501,050 |
| 35% | $250,526 - $626,350 | $501,051 - $751,600 |
| 37% | Over $626,350 | Over $751,600 |

**Net Investment Income Tax:**
- Additional 3.8% for high earners (income over $200,000 single / $250,000 married)

## H2: Strategies to Reduce Tax on Interest Income

**Strategy 1: Use Tax-Advantaged Accounts**
- Hold bonds in IRA or 401(k)
- Interest grows tax-deferred
- Pay tax only upon withdrawal (traditional) or never (Roth)

**Strategy 2: Invest in Municipal Bonds**
- Interest is federally tax-exempt
- May be state-exempt if bonds from your state
- Lower yield but after-tax return may be better

**Strategy 3: Series I and EE Savings Bonds**
- Tax deferred until redemption
- Can be tax-free if used for education (qualified expenses)

**Strategy 4: Tax Loss Harvesting**
- Offset interest income with capital losses
- Up to $3,000 net loss deductible against ordinary income
