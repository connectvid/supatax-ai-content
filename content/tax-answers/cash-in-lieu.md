## H1

Cash in Lieu: What It Means on Your Tax Return and 1099

---

## ANSWER SECTION

**Cash in lieu** (often abbreviated as **CIL** or "cash in lieu of fractional shares") is money paid to you instead of issuing fractional shares during corporate transactions like **mergers, acquisitions, stock splits, or spin-offs**. When a company action results in you being entitled to a partial share—for example, receiving 2.5 shares in a merger—the broker typically sells the fractional share (0.5) and pays you cash for its value. For 2025 tax purposes, this cash is treated as a **taxable sale of stock** and must be reported on your tax return. Your broker reports cash in lieu on **Form 1099-B**, and you must calculate the capital gain or loss by allocating a portion of your original cost basis to the fractional share that was sold.

---

## H2: When Cash in Lieu Occurs

Cash in lieu most commonly happens during these corporate actions:

**Mergers and acquisitions:**
- Company A acquires Company B
- Exchange ratio: 1.5 shares of A for each share of B
- If you owned 10 shares of B, you receive 15 shares of A—no fractional shares
- But if you owned 11 shares of B, you receive 16.5 shares
- The 0.5 share is sold, and you receive cash in lieu

**Stock splits:**
- Company announces 3-for-2 stock split
- You own 25 shares
- After split: 37.5 shares
- The 0.5 share becomes cash in lieu

**Reverse stock splits:**
- Company announces 1-for-10 reverse split
- You own 105 shares
- After split: 10.5 shares
- The 0.5 share becomes cash in lieu

**Spin-offs:**
- Parent company spins off subsidiary
- Shareholders receive fractional shares of new company
- Fractional shares are sold for cash

---

## H2: Tax Treatment of Cash in Lieu

**Taxable event:**
Cash in lieu is treated as a sale of the fractional share, creating a taxable capital gain or loss.

**Reporting requirements:**
- Report on **Schedule D** (Capital Gains and Losses)
- Transfer to **Form 8949** (Sales and Other Dispositions of Capital Assets)
- Reported by broker on **Form 1099-B**

**Character of gain/loss:**
- **Short-term:** If you held the original shares one year or less
- **Long-term:** If you held the original shares more than one year
- The holding period carries over from your original shares

**Calculating gain or loss:**
```
Cash Received (Proceeds)
- Allocated Cost Basis of Fractional Share
= Capital Gain or Loss
```

---

## H2: How to Calculate Your Cost Basis

Determining the correct cost basis for the fractional share requires allocation:

**Method 1: Pro-rata allocation**
Allocate your original cost basis proportionally to the fractional share.

**Example calculation:**
- Original shares owned: 100 shares
- Original total cost basis: $5,000 ($50 per share)
- Merger exchange ratio: 1.5 to 1
- New shares received: 150 shares
- Cash in lieu received for: 0.5 fractional share

**Allocation:**
- Fractional share portion: 0.5 ÷ 150 = 0.00333 (0.333%)
- Cost basis allocated: $5,000 × 0.00333 = **$16.67**
- Cash in lieu received: **$25.00**
- Capital gain: $25.00 - $16.67 = **$8.33**

**Method 2: Fair market value allocation**
Some brokers allocate based on fair market value at the time of the corporate action.

**Documentation needed:**
- Keep records of your original purchase
- Save corporate action notifications
- Retain Form 1099-B statements
- Document your basis calculation

## H2: Reporting Cash in Lieu on Your Tax Return

**Step 1: Receive Form 1099-B**
Your broker sends Form 1099-B showing:
- Box 1d: Proceeds (cash received)
- Box 1e: Cost basis (may be $0 or incomplete—verify!)
- Box 2: Short-term or long-term gain/loss indicator

**Step 2: Complete Form 8949**
Report each cash in lieu transaction:
- Column (a): Description ("XYZ Corp fractional share")
- Column (b) and (c): Date acquired (original purchase date)
- Column (d): Date sold (corporate action date)
- Column (e): Proceeds (cash received)
- Column (f): Cost basis (your calculated amount)
- Column (g): Adjustment code and amount (if needed)
- Column (h): Gain or loss

**Step 3: Transfer to Schedule D**
Carry totals from Form 8949 to Schedule D.

**Step 4: Include in Form 1040**
Capital gains flow to your main tax return.

---

## H2: Common Issues and Mistakes

**Incorrect basis reporting:**
Brokers sometimes report $0 basis for cash in lieu, which overstates your gain. You must calculate and report the correct allocated basis.

**Missing the transaction:**
Small cash in lieu amounts ($1-10) are easy to overlook but are still taxable and reportable.

**Wrong holding period:**
The holding period carries from your original shares. Don't use the corporate action date as your acquisition date.

**Aggregation errors:**
Each cash in lieu event is a separate transaction. Don't aggregate multiple events into one line item.

**Wash sale complications:**
If you repurchase shares of the same company within 30 days, wash sale rules may apply (though this is rare with cash in lieu).
