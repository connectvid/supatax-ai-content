## H1

What Is Tax-Exempt Interest Income?

---

## ANSWER SECTION

**Tax-exempt interest income** is interest you earn from investments that are not subject to federal income tax. The most common source is **municipal bonds** issued by state and local governments. You report this income on **Form 1040, Line 2a**. While exempt from federal tax, it may still be taxable at the state level unless you invest in bonds from your own state.

---

## H2: Common Sources of Tax-Exempt Interest

**Primary sources of tax-exempt interest:**

**Municipal bonds:** Bonds issued by states, cities, counties, and other local government entities to fund public projects like roads, schools, and utilities.

**U.S. territory bonds:** Bonds from Puerto Rico, Guam, U.S. Virgin Islands, and other territories are generally exempt from federal, state, and local taxes.

**Municipal bond mutual funds and ETFs:** Funds that invest in municipal bonds pass tax-exempt interest through to investors.

**Tax-exempt money market funds:** Money market funds that invest in short-term municipal securities.

**529 plans:** Earnings grow tax-exempt when used for qualified education expenses (though this is different from municipal bond interest).

**Not tax-exempt:** Corporate bonds, U.S. Treasury bonds (federally taxable, state-exempt), bank account interest, and CD interest are all fully taxable.

---

## H2: How to Report Tax-Exempt Interest

You must report tax-exempt interest even though it's not taxed:

**Form 1040, Line 2a:** Enter total tax-exempt interest
**Form 1040, Line 2b:** Enter taxable interest (separate line)

**Where the information comes from:**
- **Form 1099-INT:** Box 8 shows tax-exempt interest
- **Form 1099-DIV:** Box 11 shows exempt-interest dividends from mutual funds
- **Brokerage statements:** Year-end summary reports tax-exempt interest

**Why report it if it's not taxed?**
- The IRS tracks it for information purposes
- It may affect other tax calculations (Social Security taxation, Alternative Minimum Tax)

---

## H2: Federal vs. State Tax Treatment

Tax-exempt interest treatment varies by jurisdiction:

**Federal tax:** Generally exempt from federal income tax

**State tax:** Depends on the bond issuer and your residence:
- In-state municipal bonds: Tax-exempt in that state
- Out-of-state municipal bonds: Taxable in your home state
- U.S. territory bonds: Tax-exempt in all states

**California example:**
- California municipal bonds: Tax-exempt for California residents
- New York municipal bonds: Taxable for California residents

**New York example:**
- New York municipal bonds: Tax-exempt for New York residents
- California municipal bonds: Taxable for New York residents

**Triple-tax-exempt bonds:** Bonds from U.S. territories (Puerto Rico, Guam, etc.) are exempt from federal, state, and local taxes for all U.S. taxpayers regardless of residence.

---

## H2: The Social Security Taxation Trap

Tax-exempt interest can indirectly increase your taxes by affecting how much of your Social Security benefits are taxable.

**How it works:**
- Social Security benefits become taxable when your "combined income" exceeds certain thresholds
- Combined income = Adjusted Gross Income + Nontaxable interest + ½ of Social Security benefits
- **Tax-exempt interest counts as "nontaxable interest" in this calculation**

**2025 Social Security taxation thresholds:**
- Single: Up to 50% taxable if combined income $25,000-$34,000; up to 85% taxable above $34,000
- Married: Up to 50% taxable if combined income $32,000-$44,000; up to 85% taxable above $44,000

**Example:**
A retiree with $20,000 in Social Security and $30,000 in tax-exempt municipal bond interest may have up to 85% of their Social Security benefits taxed, even though the municipal bond interest itself is tax-exempt.
