## H1

Are GoFundMe Donations Tax Deductible?

---

## ANSWER SECTION

**GoFundMe donations to personal campaigns are NOT tax deductible.** When you donate to help a family with medical bills, funeral costs, or personal emergencies through a regular GoFundMe campaign, the IRS considers this a personal gift, not a charitable contribution. However, **donations through GoFundMe Charity** to verified **501(c)(3) nonprofit organizations** may be tax deductible if you itemize deductions on your tax return.

---

## H2: GoFundMe vs. GoFundMe Charity

GoFundMe operates two distinct platforms with different tax implications:

**GoFundMe (Personal Campaigns):**
- For individuals, families, and personal causes
- Donations are treated as personal gifts
- NOT tax deductible for donors
- Recipients generally don't report as income (gift tax rules apply)

**GoFundMe Charity:**
- For registered 501(c)(3) nonprofit organizations
- Donations may be tax deductible
- Organizations must be IRS-verified charities
- Donors receive tax receipts for contributions

**How to Tell the Difference:**
- GoFundMe Charity campaigns display a "Registered Charity" badge
- Regular GoFundMe campaigns show "Personal Campaign"
- When in doubt, check if the beneficiary is a registered nonprofit

---

## H2: Why Personal GoFundMe Donations Aren't Deductible

The IRS has strict requirements for charitable contributions:

**Requirements for Tax-Deductible Donations:**
1. Must be to a qualified organization (501(c)(3) nonprofit)
2. Must be a donation, not a payment for goods/services
3. Must be properly documented

**Personal GoFundMe Campaigns Fail Because:**
- The recipient is an individual, not a qualified organization
- Even if the cause is worthy, individuals cannot receive tax-deductible donations
- The IRS does not recognize gifts to individuals as charitable contributions

**Example:**
- Deductible: Donation to American Cancer Society (501(c)(3))
- NOT Deductible: Donation to a GoFundMe for a specific person's cancer treatment

Both support cancer care, but only donations to the qualified organization are deductible.

---

## H2: When GoFundMe Donations ARE Deductible

GoFundMe Charity donations may be deductible if:

**The Recipient is a 501(c)(3):**
- Churches and religious organizations
- Educational institutions
- Nonprofit hospitals
- Public charities (United Way, Red Cross, etc.)
- Private foundations (less common on GoFundMe)

**Documentation Requirements:**
- Donation of $250 or more: Written acknowledgment required
- Any amount: Bank record or receipt required
- GoFundMe Charity provides tax receipts automatically

**Deduction Limits (2025):**
- Cash donations: Up to 60% of AGI
- Appreciated property: Up to 30% of AGI
- Excess can be carried forward up to 5 years

**Important:** You must itemize deductions (Schedule A) to claim charitable contributions. If you take the standard deduction, you receive no tax benefit from charitable donations.

---

## H2: Tax Treatment for Campaign Recipients

If you receive funds from a GoFundMe campaign, the tax treatment depends on the campaign type:

**Personal Campaigns (Gifts):**
- Generally NOT taxable income to recipient
- Treated as gifts under IRC Section 102
- Gift tax may apply to donor if over $18,000 per recipient (2025)
- Exception: If providing goods/services in exchange, may be taxable income

**Charitable Campaigns:**
- 501(c)(3) organizations report donations as tax-exempt revenue
- Must follow IRS rules for charitable organizations
- Cannot benefit private individuals

**Business Campaigns:**
- If raising capital for business, may be taxable
- Could be treated as income, loans, or equity depending on structure

---

## H2: State Tax Considerations

**California:**
- Follows federal rules
- Personal GoFundMe donations not deductible
- GoFundMe Charity donations to 501(c)(3)s deductible if you itemize
- California standard deduction: $5,363 single / $10,726 married (2025)

**New York:**
- Similar to federal treatment
- Must itemize on state return to claim charitable deductions

**Texas, Florida, Washington:**
- No state income tax
- State deduction question doesn't apply

Most states follow federal treatment for charitable contributions.
