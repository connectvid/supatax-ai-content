## H1

Is Social Security Taxable in California?

---

## ANSWER SECTION

**Social Security is NOT taxable in California.** California is one of approximately **38 states** that completely exempt Social Security retirement, disability, and survivor benefits from state income tax. This means whether you receive $1,000 or $3,000 per month in Social Security benefits, you pay **$0 California state tax** on that income. However, your Social Security benefits may still be subject to **federal income tax** depending on your total combined income from all sources.

---

## H2: California State Tax Treatment

**California Fully Exempts:**
- Social Security retirement benefits
- Social Security disability benefits (SSDI)
- Social Security survivor benefits
- Supplemental Security Income (SSI) — also federally exempt

**How to Report on California Return:**
- Report Social Security benefits on Schedule CA
- California subtraction eliminates state tax
- Line 5 subtraction means benefits are not included in California taxable income

**Form 540 Schedule CA:**
- Enter Social Security benefits
- California subtraction adjustment
- Result: $0 California tax on benefits

---

## H2: Federal Taxation of Social Security

While California doesn't tax your benefits, the federal government might:

**Combined Income Formula:**
Combined Income = Adjusted Gross Income + Nontaxable Interest + ½ of Social Security Benefits

**2025 Federal Taxation Thresholds:**

| Filing Status | 50% Taxable | 85% Taxable |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| Single | $25,000 - $34,000 | Over $34,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $32,000 - $44,000 | Over $44,000 |

**How Much Is Taxable:**
- Below lower threshold: 0% taxable
- Between thresholds: Up to 50% taxable
- Above upper threshold: Up to 85% taxable
- Maximum 85% of benefits can be taxed

**Example - Single Filer:**
- Social Security: $24,000/year
- ½ of SS: $12,000
- Other income (pension, 401k): $15,000
- Tax-exempt interest: $2,000
- Combined income: $29,000
- Result: Up to 50% of SS may be taxable federally

## H2: Strategies to Minimize Federal Tax on Social Security

**Strategy 1: Manage Withdrawals**
- Delay 401(k) and IRA withdrawals
- Use Roth IRA accounts (withdrawals don't count toward combined income)
- Time large withdrawals across multiple years

**Strategy 2: Roth Conversions Before Age 62**
- Convert traditional IRA to Roth IRA before taking Social Security
- Pay tax now, reduce future combined income
- Roth withdrawals don't count toward SS taxation formula

**Strategy 3: Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)**
- If 70½ or older, donate IRA distributions directly to charity
- QCDs don't count as taxable income
- Reduces combined income for SS taxation purposes

**Strategy 4: Tax-Efficient Investment Placement**
- Hold taxable bonds in tax-deferred accounts
- Hold stocks/municipal bonds in taxable accounts
- Manage capital gains timing
