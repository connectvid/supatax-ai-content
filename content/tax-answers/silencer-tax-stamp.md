Silencer Tax Stamp: Same as Suppressor Stamp — Here's the Process

---

## ANSWER SECTION

A **silencer tax stamp** is the exact same document as a suppressor tax stamp—there is no difference. The terms "silencer" and "suppressor" both refer to the same firearm accessory that reduces muzzle noise. In 2025, obtaining a silencer tax stamp requires submitting **ATF Form 4** with a **$200 payment**, fingerprints, photos, and undergoing a background check. The approval process takes **6 to 12 months**. The National Firearms Act uses "silencer" in its legal text, while "suppressor" is the technically more accurate term preferred by manufacturers and enthusiasts.

---

## H2: Silencer vs. Suppressor: Same Thing, Different Terms

**Historical context:** Hiram Maxim invented the device in 1902 and patented it as the "Maxim Silencer." This is why the National Firearms Act of 1934 uses the term "silencer" in its legal definition.

**Modern terminology:** The firearms industry prefers "suppressor" because these devices don't actually silence a gunshot—they only suppress the noise to safer hearing levels. A suppressed gunshot is still approximately 120-140 decibels.

**Legal usage:** All ATF forms, federal laws, and court documents use "silencer." When filling out ATF Form 4, you must use the legal terminology even if you personally prefer "suppressor."

**Common usage:** Both terms are widely understood and accepted. Dealers, manufacturers, and shooters use them interchangeably in conversation.

---

## H2: Silencer Tax Stamp Requirements

The process for obtaining a silencer tax stamp is identical to the suppressor process:

**Cost:** $200 federal tax per silencer
**Primary form:** ATF Form 4 (Application for Tax Paid Transfer)
**Additional requirements:**
- Two FD-258 fingerprint cards
- Two passport photos (2x2 inches)
- Copy sent to local Chief Law Enforcement Officer (CLEO)

**Eligibility:**
- Must be at least 21 years old
- U.S. citizen or legal permanent resident
- Legally eligible to possess firearms (no disqualifying criminal history)
- Resident of a state where silencers are legal (42 states)

**Wait time:** 6-12 months for paper applications, 3-6 months for eForms

---

## H2: States Where Silencers Are Legal

Silencers are legal with a tax stamp in **42 states** and prohibited in **8 states plus D.C.:**

**Legal states include:**
- Texas, Florida, Arizona, Ohio, Pennsylvania
- Georgia, North Carolina, Tennessee, Alabama
- Most western and southern states

**Prohibited states:**
- California, New York, New Jersey, Massachusetts
- Illinois, Hawaii, Rhode Island, Delaware
- Washington D.C.

**Special restrictions:**
- **Vermont:** Legal but cannot use while hunting
- **Connecticut:** Legal with additional state permit

**Important:** Even with a federal tax stamp, possessing a silencer in a prohibited state is a felony punishable by up to 10 years in prison.

## H2: The Silencer Registration Process

**Step 1: Purchase from an FFL dealer**
Select your silencer and pay for it. It remains at the dealer during the approval process.

**Step 2: Complete ATF Form 4**
Fill out the transfer application with your information and the silencer's details (manufacturer, model, serial number).

**Step 3: Get fingerprinted**
Obtain two FD-258 fingerprint cards from your local police station or authorized provider.

**Step 4: Take passport photos**
Get two 2x2 inch photos taken within the last 6 months.

**Step 5: Submit application and $200**
Mail or eFile your complete application with the tax payment.

**Step 6: Wait for approval**
The ATF conducts a background check and processes your application.

**Step 7: Take possession**
Once approved, complete the transfer at your dealer with Form 4473.

| Short-barreled rifle | $200 | Form 1 or 4 | Barrel under 16" |
| Short-barreled shotgun | $200 | Form 1 or 4 | Barrel under 18" |
| Machine gun | $200 | Form 4 | Pre-1986 only |
| Destructive device | $200 | Form 4 or 1 | Includes grenades |

Silencers are the most commonly registered NFA items because they're relatively affordable (starting around $300-500), have practical hearing protection benefits, and are legal in most states.
