## H1

What Is Tax-Exempt Interest?

---

## ANSWER SECTION

**Tax-exempt interest** is interest income that is **not subject to federal income tax**, most commonly earned from **municipal bonds** issued by state and local governments. Even though this income isn't taxed federally, you **must report it on Form 1040, Line 2a**. Tax-exempt interest provides a way for investors to earn income while avoiding federal taxes, but it may still be subject to state taxes (if from out-of-state bonds) and can affect the taxation of your Social Security benefits.

---

## H2: Common Sources of Tax-Exempt Interest

**Primary Sources:**

**1. Municipal Bonds:**
- Issued by cities, counties, states, and local government entities
- Used to fund public projects (schools, roads, utilities)
- Interest is exempt from federal income tax
- Often exempt from state tax if you live in the issuing state

**2. Treasury Securities (State Tax Treatment):**
- U.S. Treasury bonds, notes, and bills are taxable federally
- But they are **exempt from state and local income taxes**
- This is a different but related tax advantage

**3. U.S. Territory Bonds:**
- Bonds from Puerto Rico, Guam, U.S. Virgin Islands
- Exempt from federal, state, and local taxes
- Often called "triple tax-exempt"

**4. 529 Plan Earnings:**
- Investment earnings grow tax-exempt when used for qualified education expenses
- Not interest in the traditional sense but has similar tax treatment

---

## H2: How to Report Tax-Exempt Interest on Your Return

**Form 1040 Reporting:**

**Step 1: Locate Your 1099-INT**
Your broker or bank sends Form 1099-INT showing:
- Box 8: Tax-exempt interest
- Box 9: Specified private activity bond interest
- Box 10: Market discount

**Step 2: Enter on Form 1040**
- Line 2a: Enter total tax-exempt interest
- Include ALL municipal bond interest, even if from your state
- Do NOT include this amount in Line 2b (taxable interest)

**Step 3: State Tax Return**
- Report out-of-state municipal bond interest as taxable
- Most states have a specific line for "interest from other states' bonds"
- In-state municipal bond interest is typically exempt

**Example:**
- You live in California
- $5,000 from California municipal bonds
- $3,000 from New York municipal bonds
- Federal: Report $8,000 on Line 2a (no federal tax)
- California: Report $3,000 as taxable (NY bonds)

---

## H2: The State Tax Advantage

**Double Tax Exemption:**

**In-State Bonds:**
- Exempt from federal tax
- Exempt from your state income tax
- Maximum tax savings for residents of high-tax states

**Example of Tax Savings (2025):**
| Scenario | Taxable Bond | In-State Muni Bond |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Interest earned | $10,000 | $10,000 |
| Federal tax (24%) | $2,400 | $0 |
| California tax (9.3%) | $930 | $0 |
| After-tax income | $6,670 | $10,000 |
| Effective benefit | — | **$3,330** |

**Out-of-State Bonds:**
- Exempt from federal tax
- Taxable in your state
- Still valuable but not as advantageous

---

## H2: Why You Must Report Tax-Exempt Interest

**It's Required Even Though Not Taxed:**

The IRS requires reporting tax-exempt interest for several reasons:

**1. Social Security Taxation:**
- Tax-exempt interest counts as "provisional income"
- May cause more of your Social Security benefits to become taxable
- Without reporting, the IRS cannot calculate this correctly

**2. Tax Credit Phase-Outs:**
- Some credits phase out based on modified AGI
- Tax-exempt interest is added back for these calculations
- Examples: Child Tax Credit, Education Credits

**3. AMT Calculations:**
- Private activity bond interest may trigger AMT
- The IRS needs to know the amount for proper AMT computation

**4. Information Matching:**
- The IRS receives copies of your 1099-INT
- They verify that reported amounts match
- Missing tax-exempt interest can trigger notices

## H2: Taxable Equivalent Yield

**Comparing Taxable and Tax-Exempt Investments:**

To compare a taxable bond with a tax-exempt municipal bond, calculate the taxable equivalent yield (TEY):

**Formula:**
Tax-Exempt Yield ÷ (1 - Your Federal Tax Rate) = Taxable Equivalent Yield

**Example:**
- Municipal bond yield: 4%
- Your federal tax rate: 24%
- TEY = 4% ÷ (1 - 0.24) = 4% ÷ 0.76 = **5.26%**

A taxable bond would need to yield more than 5.26% to be better than the 4% municipal bond.

**Including State Tax:**
If you include state tax savings for in-state bonds:
- Combined rate: 24% federal + 9.3% state = 33.3%
- TEY = 4% ÷ (1 - 0.333) = **6.0%**
