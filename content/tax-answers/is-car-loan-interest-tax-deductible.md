## H1

Is Car Loan Interest Tax Deductible?

---

## ANSWER SECTION

**Car loan interest is only tax deductible if you use the vehicle for business purposes.** Personal vehicle loan interest is **not deductible**—the IRS eliminated this deduction decades ago. For business use, you can deduct the portion of car loan interest that corresponds to your business use percentage. If you use your car 60% for business, you can deduct 60% of the annual loan interest. This deduction is claimed on **Schedule C** for self-employed individuals or as an unreimbursed employee expense (currently suspended for W-2 employees through 2025).

---

## H2: Personal vs. Business Use Rules

**Personal Use:**

| Scenario | Deductible? | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Commuting to work | No | Never deductible |
| Personal errands | No | Not business use |
| Family car | No | Personal expense |
| Second car for pleasure | No | Personal expense |

**Business Use (Deductible):**

| Scenario | Deductible? | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Self-employed business travel | Yes | Schedule C |
| Rideshare driving (Uber/Lyft) | Yes | Business use |
| Delivery driver | Yes | Business use |
| Real estate agent showings | Yes | Business use |
| Sales calls | Yes | Business use |

**W-2 Employees:**
- Car loan interest **not deductible** for W-2 employees (2018-2025)
- Suspended by Tax Cuts and Jobs Act
- May return in 2026 unless extended

## H2: Calculating Business Use Percentage

**Record Keeping Requirements:**

To claim car loan interest deduction, you must track:
- Total miles driven during year
- Business miles driven
- Date, destination, purpose of business trips
- Starting and ending odometer readings

**Business Use Formula:**
```
Business Miles ÷ Total Miles = Business Use Percentage
```

**Example:**
- Total miles: 15,000
- Business miles: 9,000
- Business use: 60%
- Personal use: 40%

**Documentation Methods:**

**Mileage Log (Required):**
- Physical notebook
- Spreadsheet
- Mobile apps (MileIQ, Everlance, Stride)

**What to Record:**
- Date of trip
- Starting location
- Destination
- Business purpose
- Miles driven

## H2: Two Methods to Deduct Car Expenses

**Method 1: Standard Mileage Rate**

**2025 Rate:** $0.70 per business mile

**What It Covers:**
- Gas and oil
- Maintenance and repairs
- Depreciation
- Insurance
- Registration fees
- **NOT car loan interest separately**

**When Interest Is Included:**
The standard mileage rate includes an allowance for vehicle costs, so you **cannot** separately deduct loan interest using this method.

**Best For:**
- Lower-priced vehicles
- Low mileage drivers
- Simple record keeping

**Method 2: Actual Expense Method**

**What You Can Deduct (Business Portion Only):**
- Gas and oil
- Repairs and maintenance
- Tires
- Insurance
- Registration fees and licenses
- **Car loan interest**
- Lease payments (if applicable)
- Depreciation

**Car Loan Interest Calculation:**

**Example:**
- Annual car loan interest: $1,200
- Business use percentage: 60%
- Deductible interest: $1,200 × 60% = **$720**

**Best For:**
- Expensive vehicles
- High mileage drivers
- When actual costs exceed standard rate

---

## H2: How to Claim the Deduction

**For Self-Employed (Schedule C):**

**Step 1: Choose Your Method**
- Decide between standard mileage or actual expenses
- Must use actual expense method to deduct loan interest

**Step 2: Calculate Business Percentage**
- Complete mileage log
- Calculate business use percentage

**Step 3: Calculate Deductible Interest**
- Annual loan interest × business percentage
- Keep loan statements as proof

**Step 4: Report on Schedule C**
- Line 16b: "Other interest" OR
- Include with other actual expenses
- Total car expenses go on Line 9

**Required Records:**
- Mileage log for entire year
- Loan statements showing interest paid
- Receipts for other car expenses (actual method)

## H2: Special Situations

**Vehicle Financed Through Dealer vs. Bank:**

**Bank Loan:**
- Interest clearly stated on Form 1098
- Easy to identify amount
- Deductible portion straightforward

**Dealer Financing:**
- May be harder to separate interest from principal
- Request amortization schedule
- Interest is still deductible business portion

**Leased Vehicles:**
- Lease payments deductible (business portion)
- No loan interest (you don't own the vehicle)
- Different calculation than owned vehicles

**Heavy Vehicles (Over 6,000 lbs):**
- May qualify for Section 179 deduction
- Can deduct larger portion in first year
- Special rules apply for SUVs and trucks

**Electric Vehicles:**
- May qualify for EV tax credit
- Credit applies separately from expense deduction
- Business use percentage applies to credit too
